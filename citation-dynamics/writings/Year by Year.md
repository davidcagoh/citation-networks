[Scopus 1900–2020: Growth in articles, abstracts, countries, fields, and journals | Quantitative Science Studies | MIT Press](https://direct.mit.edu/qss/article/3/1/37/109076/Scopus-1900-2020-Growth-in-articles-abstracts)

Scientometric research often relies on large-scale bibliometric databases of academic journal articles. Long-term and longitudinal research can be affected if the composition of a database varies over time, and text processing research can be affected if the percentage of articles with abstracts changes. This article therefore assesses changes in the magnitude of the coverage of a major citation index, Scopus, over 121 years from 1900\. The results show sustained exponential growth from 1900, except for dips during both world wars, and with increased growth after 2004\. Over the same period, the percentage of articles with 500+ character abstracts increased from 1% to 95%. The number of different journals in Scopus also increased exponentially, but slowing down from 2010, with the number of articles per journal being approximately constant until 1980, then tripling due to megajournals and online-only publishing. The breadth of Scopus, in terms of the number of narrow fields with substantial numbers of articles, simultaneously increased from one field having 1,000 articles in 1945 to 308 fields in 2020\. Scopus’s international character also radically changed from 68% of first authors from Germany and the United States in 1900 to just 17% in 2020, with China dominating (25%).

During World War II, the U.S. government launched an unprecedented effort to mobi- lize science for war: the newly-established Office of Scientific Research and Development (OSRD) entered thousands of R\&D contracts with industrial and academic contractors, spending one to two orders of magnitude more than what the government was previously investing in science. In this pa- per, we study the long-run effects of the OSRD-supported research effort on U.S. invention. Using data on all OSRD contracts, we show that these investments had large effects on the direction and location of U.S. invention and high-tech industrial employment, setting in motion agglomeration forces which shaped the technology clusters of the postwar era. Our results demonstrate the effects of a large, mission-driven government R\&D program on the growth of domestic technology clusters and long-run technological progress.

**Analysis**

1940s: 

The dip in APS publications from 1940 to 1947 aligns closely with the disruptions of World War II, which had significant impacts on scientific research and publishing:

	1\.	Wartime Prioritization and Redirection of Resources: During World War II, many scientists and researchers in the U.S., Europe, and Asia shifted their focus to military-related projects, such as radar, cryptography, and nuclear physics (e.g., the Manhattan Project). This redirection meant fewer resources, personnel, and time were available for conventional scientific research and publications.  
	2\.	International Disruptions: Many European scientists faced obstacles due to occupation, displacement, or restricted access to academic facilities, and several prominent scientists emigrated to the U.S. or went into hiding. Communication and collaboration across borders were hindered, reducing the overall scientific output and publication rate.  
	3\.	Material Shortages and Publishing Constraints: Wartime paper rationing and material shortages constrained publishing capacities. Scientific journals had to reduce their page counts or frequency of issues, limiting the number of articles they could publish.  
	4\.	Post-War Recovery: After 1945, it took a few years for scientific communities to reorganize, rebuild institutions, and shift back to peacetime research priorities. Recovery in funding, infrastructure, and international collaboration gradually allowed publication levels to return to pre-war numbers by around 1947\.

These disruptions during the war years would have created a noticeable decline in publication counts, with full recovery only possible after several years of re-establishing regular scientific and publishing activities.

1950s:

During the 1950s and 1960s, the tremendous and rapid growth in American physics departments resulted in lack of office space, overcrowded laboratory con- ditions, proliferation of new bureaucratic procedures, a widespread feelings of “facelessness,” and a perilous loss of “intimacy.” Routine social events, such as the annual picnics of Berkeley’s physics department, became logistically exhaust- ing affairs.10 The frequent complaints about overcrowding, from both students and faculty, may explain the flattening of the physics Ph.D.-curve during the 1950s despite strong demand. Enrollments had swollen to over three times their prewar highs, far faster than the departments’ faculties and physical plant. Despite a boom in new physics buildings and building extensions, department chairs across the country complained bitterly about their lack of adequate space.11 The next dra- matic rise in the annual output of physics Ph.D.s, after Sputnik, was accomplished primarily by increasing the number of doctoral institutions, rather than by squeez- ing more students into the existing graduate programs: the number of institutions granting Ph.D.s in physics within the United States doubled between 1959 and 1967\.12 The number of physics Ph.D.s granted each year jumped by a factor of three, to over 1,500 by the late 1960s. (According to David Kaiser, 2002\)

1970s:  
 The drop in American Physical Society (APS) publications during the   
1970s, much like the dip during WWII, was influenced by a complex mix of   
economic, social, and academic factors. One primary cause in the 1970s was   
a significant downturn in physics job opportunities, known as the "job   
crisis" in physics. This issue led to decreased enrollment in physics   
Ph.D. programs as students sought careers with more stable job prospects.   
At the time, there was widespread concern about the surplus of physicists,   
which created a domino effect on the number of new research submissions   
and publications. Additionally, the Vietnam War era and the growing social   
awareness among scientists prompted APS to address issues like employment   
and diversity, leading to new programs for career support and advocacy but   
possibly diverting some focus from traditional academic research   
publication【804†source】【805†source】.

This decline was further compounded by the gradual shift in physics   
research priorities and resources, as government funding began to favor   
other fields, including space and defense technology. APS adjusted over   
time, especially in the 1980s and 1990s, as it introduced initiatives like   
the Forum on Industrial and Applied Physics to better align with the   
changing employment landscape for physicists and increase academic   
engagement outside traditional research avenues.

Several resources discuss the decline in physics job opportunities during   
the early 1970s, which heavily impacted publication output in physics.   
David Kaiser’s work, “Cold War Requisitions,” notes that by the late   
1960s, oversupply and shifts in military funding led to fewer jobs in   
physics. This caused decreased enrollments and cutbacks in research, as   
universities and agencies reduced hiring and budgets in response to the   
economic slowdown and reallocated resources from physics to other   
disciplines and applications, like computer science【811†source】.

For detailed historical context, Kaiser’s article and Jeff Hecht’s report   
on physics funding provide in-depth discussions of these trends.

2015: 

The dip in publications from the American Physical Society (APS) around   
2015 could be attributed to several factors affecting the broader academic   
publishing landscape. One possible reason is the increase in competition   
from open-access journals, which began to significantly change the   
publication dynamics around that time. Many researchers started to prefer   
platforms that provided greater visibility and access to their work   
without paywalls.

Additionally, the APS has undergone various changes in its journal   
offerings and editorial policies, which may have influenced submission   
rates. For example, the introduction of new journals, such as \*Physical   
Review X\* (an open-access journal), could have shifted where researchers   
chose to submit their work.

There has also been a notable increase in the emphasis on   
interdisciplinary research and collaboration in recent years, potentially   
leading to a fragmentation of submission pools as researchers explore new   
fields and platforms.
